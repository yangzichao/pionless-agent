# Source Policy

## Primary

Prefer primary sources first.

- Official documentation, specifications, RFCs, standards.
- First-party announcements, release notes, changelogs.
- Peer-reviewed papers, preprints from established venues.
- Regulatory filings, court records, government datasets.
- Vendor docs and benchmark authors.
- Source code, commit history, issue trackers.

## Secondary

Use secondary sources for synthesis or market framing, **not** as the sole support for important claims.

- Major news outlets with disclosed editorial standards.
- Reference works (encyclopedias, handbooks).
- Survey papers citing primary sources.

## Active disagreement-seeking

Pro tier requires **actively seeking sources that disagree** with the emerging thesis. Confirmation bias is the main failure mode at this tier. Every major claim should be searched against an explicit "is this wrong?" query angle (`references/retrieval-policy.md`).

## Recency

For time-sensitive topics, verify current facts rather than relying on memory. Cite the source date on every reference.

## Inference vs sourced fact

Always distinguish what a source said from what you are inferring. Use phrases like "based on [source]" for sourced facts and "this suggests" for inference.
