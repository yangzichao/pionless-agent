# Source Policy

Three tiers, in order of preference.

## Primary

First-party material. Use whenever available.

- Official documentation, specifications, RFCs, standards.
- First-party announcements, release notes, changelogs.
- Peer-reviewed papers, preprints from established venues.
- Regulatory filings, court records, government datasets.
- Source code, commit history, issue trackers.

## Authoritative secondary

Established outlets and well-cited surveys with a track record. Acceptable for context and corroboration; usually not sufficient as the sole support for a load-bearing numeric or factual claim.

- Major news outlets with disclosed editorial standards.
- Reference works (encyclopedias, handbooks).
- Survey papers citing primary sources.

## Weak

Use for orientation only. Never the sole source for a load-bearing claim.

- Personal blogs, forum threads, social posts.
- AI-generated summaries.
- Marketing pages without first-party citations.

## Recency

For time-sensitive topics, prefer sources dated within the last 6 months. For structural or definitional claims, age is acceptable; cite the source date regardless.

## Inference vs sourced fact

Always distinguish what a source said from what you are inferring. Use phrases like "based on [source]" for sourced facts and "this suggests" for inference.

## Conflicts

When sources disagree, surface the conflict in the report. Pick the source closest to primary; never silently discard the dissenting source.
